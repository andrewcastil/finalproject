Abstract

Imagination, often celebrated for its role in creativity and development, can also intersect with maladaptive personality traits, echoing the archetype of the ‘tormented genius.’ This study examines the structure of imagination, analyzing a large-scale cross-sectional dataset (N = 10,000) with a planned-missingness design. The functional sample size (pairwise-n = 8,500) was derived from the mean number of pairwise item administrations in the item pool. Our analysis found four factors emerge, which have been titled Hopelessness, Anxiety, Resentment, and Shame. These results highlight the dynamic, multi-faceted nature of imagination, underscoring its contributions to positive mental health and the development and maintenance of personality pathology.

Introduction

Imagination is a fundamental human ability which permeates our daily experiences, influencing everything from the mundane to the grand narratives of myths and legends. Central to creativity, problem-solving, and foresight, imagination shapes our perceptions of reality and the cognitive schemas we use to interpret the world around us (Gotlieb et al. 2019). As a multifaceted construct, imagination involves varying levels of mental imagery, social cognition, mental simulation, emotion, and temporal exploration (Abraham 2016), using combinations of existing memory to create an experience distinct from external sensory stimuli. Though widely recognized as a positively valanced construct, imagination plays a complex role in mental health and personality. The tendency to frequently and intensely envision negative future scenarios is a feature of clinical depression (Gotlib and Joormann 2010) and anxiety (Wu et al. 2015), while difficulty in distinguishing imagination from reality plays a role in thought disorders such as schizophrenia (Rasmussen et al. 2022). The intersection of imagination with psychopathology highlights the challenges associated with measuring such a complex construct and evaluating its impacts on mental health.

The Imaginative Process

Imagination is a cognitive function that encompasses various processes and is integral to human psychological functioning across a range of contexts. Abraham (2016) identifies five core categories that define imagination: perceptual/motor-related mental imagery, intentionality or recollective processing, novel combinatorial or generative processing, aesthetic phenomenology, and altered psychological states. This is extended by Crespi (2020), who asserts there are seven major components of imagination: pretend play, creativity, narratives and aesthetics, mental time travel, salience, mental imagery and sensory systems, and a neural system enabling imagination. These categories, grounded in both philosophical theory and empirical neuroscience evidence, highlight imagination’s virtually limitless capacity and varied manifestations. Each category has a distinct neural basis and operates through separate cognitive systems, contributing to the multifaceted nature of imaginative cognition. Common processes, such as mental simulation (Markman, Klein, and Suhr 2012), mental time travel, and perspective-taking, involve dynamic interplay between these categories. This interplay allows individuals to transcend the immediate present with novel thoughts about possible, past, and future scenarios.

Mullally and Maguire (2014) emphasize that memory forms the foundation for such imaginative projections, enabling the construction of future scenarios based on past experiences. This predictive aspect of imagination (“foresight”) is crucial for adaptive functioning, often aligned with personal and social objectives through cognitive control mechanisms (Sass and Schuldberg 2011). While the neuroscientific understanding of some aspects of imagination, such as imagery, is well-developed, others like altered states remain less explored. One key area in this ongoing exploration is the Default Mode Network (Buckner, Andrews-Hanna, and Schacter 2008). Continual research into the facets of imagination is poised to uncover new insights and relationships. However, it’s crucial to recognize that dysfunction in imagination, such as excessive daydreaming (Somer 2002) or problematic psychological states (Abraham 2016) can be maladaptive. This underscores the importance of imagination as both a cognitive function and an enduring source of individual differences across the lifespan.

The evolution in psychometric research towards multifactor, dimensional models reflects a significant shift in how imagination is measured. Historically, tools like the Torrance Tests of Creative Thinking (Torrance 1966) and Gough’s Creative Personality Scale (Gough 1979) approached assessing imagination as a component of creativity. However, recent advancements have led to direct measures of imagination as a standalone construct. Notable advancements include the Imaginal Processes Inventory (Singer and Antrobus 1963), a cross-cultural measure by Feng et al. (2017), and dimensional scales such as the Four-Factor Imagination Scale (Zabelina and Condon 2020). However, the need for direct and clinically useful assessment instruments remains.

The Current Study

This study aims to explore the structure of maladaptive imagination. Specifically, the primary objective is to develop a psychometric assessment instrument that captures the imaginative processes that inflict harm on mental health and well-being.

Examining this relationship will clarify the extent to which imagination can prove maladaptive in various contexts. For example, in affective disorders like depression, imaginative processes may contribute to persistent negative anticipatory thinking and catastrophizing, acting as a feature of its symptomatology. In thought disorders such as schizophrenia, dysfunctional imaginative processes could relate to difficulties in distinguishing between internal imaginings and external reality. In the realm of Axis II disorders, persistent imaginative patterns that result in maladaptive decision making and elevated instability in affect may underpin certain personality disorders. This confluence between imaginative processes and personality pathology highlights the potential for enhancing existing therapeutic methodologies by integrating imagination. Clinical approaches such as Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and Art Therapy already target malformed cognitions and leverage imaginative expression as therapeutic tools (Malchiodi 2011) and could further benefit from the insights of this research by targeting specific maladaptive imaginative processes

Hypotheses

We hypothesize there will be four distinct factors of imagination that emerge. Anxiety, defined by imagination that incurs persistent worry, rumination, or stress; Hopelessness, where imagination which results in imagination about the future devoid of positive affect; Resentment, in which imagination produces externally oriented negative affect resulting in antisocial thoughts and behaviors; Shame, where imagination is a feature in negative perceptions of the self and persistent feelings on inadequacy.

Method

To prepare our data for analysis, we needed to simulate it. This was achieved using rnorm() and round(). The four factors were simulated separately in “blocks” of 25 items each and merged together. These blocks had different, randomly selected means and standard deviations in order to allow the factor analysis to locate the desired clusters of items from each of the factors. A spattering of NAs were introduced using something like this: sample(1:n, 0.15nrow(data)ncol(data), replace = T) paired with a for loop. This introduced some amount of missingness (in this case, 15%) to make the data more realistic and add a bit of intrigue and decision making around handling the missing data. After this process was completed, the data were ready for analysis including imputation, removal of missing data, partitioning into holdouts, and more. In this stage, we used mutate() and other tidyverse() functions per the project requirements.

Data was simulated using a random sampling procedure. Four latent factors consisting of items rated 1 - 6 were produced using the sample() function and stored into a dataframe.

In order to do a proper factor analysis, we had to handle missingness in the data. 15% of the data was missing and there were no complete rows. In order to address this missingness, imptuation was used with the makeX() function from the glmnet() package.

We visualized the data in multiple ways, including a correlation table, heatmap, and other plots. In the heatmap (first figure from the top, yellow), we can see that all the items seem to correlate with each other to some degree (multicolinearity). This is puzzling, since we would expect individual factors to emerge from the data. Therefore, we completed a series of other correlation plots (in blue) to show that we in fact have 4 unique clusters of items, each measuring a distinct aspect of maladaptive imagination. The first one, is a correlation plot of all 100 items. The second plot shows this same plot but now grouped by factor, where each red square is a factor. Finally, each factor is plotted separately to show how items within a factor correlate with each other.

In the above four-tile correlation plot, the displayed factors clockwise from the top left are: 1) Anxiety, 2) Hopelessness, 3) Resentment, 4) Shame.

Lastly, we added a proportion of variance explained plot to show how well the factors account for the variance in the response data. From this figure, we can see that each factor explained additional and about equal variance.

The above plot visualizes mean score for each item grouped by factor. This plot provides an assurance that each item is an appropriate measure by demonstrating a similarity in mean response for items across each factor. If there were a deviation from a uniform distribution in this plot, it would be an indication that (a) certain item(s) should not be used. This plot shows that, on average, individuals tended to agree a bit more with resentment-related statements and disagree a bit more with hopeless and shame-related statements.

Discussion

In this study, we observed meaningful patterns of associations between the pool of imagination items administered, substantiating the hypothesis that imagination can prove maladaptive in various contexts and that it can be quantified in a four-factor structure. 25 items loaded on to each respective factor of Anxiety, Hopelessness, Resentment, and Shame.

These findings align with prior research (Castillo and Condon 2023) which show imaginative emotional valence is strongly associated with pathological personality traits, suggesting a clinically significant link between imagination and affective psychopathology. This is particularly the case for the emergent factors of Shame and Hopelessness, which are defined by emotionally saturated items (e.g. “I see no hope for the future” or “I feel ashamed when I picture myself in comparison to others”).

Similarly, the Anxiety factor is similar to the Frequency dimension of the FFIS, revolving around persistent intrusive thoughts which impede adaptive behaviors and can result in perceptual dysregulation. The observed patterns in this study mirrored the patterns observed in Castillo and Condon (2023). These associations point to the potential of excessive engagement in imagination in contributing to thought disorders (Andrews-Hanna and Grilli 2021), indicating imaginative frequency is of clinical significance. These findings reinforce the clinical relevance of imaginative frequency and anxiety induced by imagination. Of the four emergent factors, resentment demonstrated the weakest psychometric properties, which may be attributable to the factor’s association with antisocial traits like the Dark Triad. Aggression and imagination are known to correlate with opposing constructs (imagination with openness, aggression with limited openness) and this may be resulting in attenuation effects. The Dark Triad literature (Kay and Arrow 2022) suggests a link with Machiavellianism, relating to adopting a socially predatory strategy. Our findings do support this, suggesting resentment may play into plotting revenge and attempting to set verbal “traps” for others.

Conclusion

Overall, these results highlight the clinical potential of these imagination factors in assessing psychopathology and treating its symptoms and underlying causes. The ability of these models to predict the relationship between imagination and personality pathology points to the importance of considering imaginative processes in clinical assessments and interventions.
